Triad Home Styles & Wi-Fi Performance

Homes in the Triad come in all shapes and materials – brick ranches, two-story colonials, modern open-concept builds, and historic plaster-walled homes. Each behaves differently when it comes to Wi-Fi. Here’s how your home’s style might be affecting your signal – and what we usually do about it.

Brick Ranch Homes

Single-story brick ranches are everywhere around Davie County. They’re great to live in, but brick and long, stretched-out layouts can be rough on Wi-Fi.

  • Challenge: Brick and masonry soak up Wi-Fi. A router at one end of a long ranch often leaves the far bedrooms starving.
  • Typical Fix: Move the main router toward the center of the home and add a second access point or wired mesh node toward the far end.

Two-Story Homes

In two-story homes, especially when the router lives on the first floor in a corner, upstairs rooms can suffer – the signal has to punch through floors, insulation, and sometimes ductwork.

  • Challenge: Vertical separation and dense floor/ceiling materials make upstairs coverage spotty.
  • Typical Fix: At least one access point per floor, ideally wired. We often mount a ceiling AP upstairs roughly above the main router/AP downstairs.

Older / Historic Homes

Older homes with plaster walls (especially with metal lath) or stone interior walls are some of the toughest environments for Wi-Fi.

  • Challenge: Plaster + metal lath and thick stone can almost block Wi-Fi entirely between rooms.
  • Typical Fix: Multiple wired access points. We carefully run cable to key locations and install low-profile APs so the network works without ruining the character of the home.

Modern Open-Concept Homes

Newer open-concept builds often look easy for Wi-Fi – fewer walls – but huge spaces, tall ceilings, and energy-efficient windows can still cause problems.

  • Challenge: Long distances to lofts or bonus rooms, Low-E coated windows reflecting signal, and lots of smart devices competing for airspace.
  • Typical Fix: One strong central AP plus a second AP or wired mesh node for distant areas, with careful channel planning on 5 GHz or 6 GHz bands.

Common Wi-Fi Killers In Any Home

Regardless of style, some things almost always hurt Wi-Fi:

  • Routers hidden behind TVs, in cabinets, or next to giant metal appliances.
  • Fish tanks, mirrors, and big metal furniture right in the signal path.
  • Neighboring networks all stacked on the same channel.

Part of every RezNet visit is hunting down these hidden signal killers and fixing what we can – by moving hardware, tuning channels, or adding a better-placed access point.
